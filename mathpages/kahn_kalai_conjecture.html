Introduction

Let \(X\) be a set, and \(p \in [0,1]\), the \(\textbf{product measure}\), \(\mu_p\), on \(2^X\) is defined as: \[\mu_p(A) = p^{|A|}(1-p)^{|X-A|}\].
Let \(X\) be a set, a subset \(\mathbf{F} \subset 2^X \) is called an increasing family if \[A \subset B , A \in \mathbf{F} \rightarrow B\in \mathbf{F} \]
(lemma 1) Given a set \(X\) and and an incresing family, \(\emptyset \neq F \subset 2^X\), then \(\mu_p(F) := \sum_{A \in F} \mu_p(A) \) is strictly increasing in \(p\).

Given a matrix \(M\), there are vectors \(v\) for which, if you multiply \(v\) by \(M\), it has the effect of stretch \(v\) by an amount \(\lambda\). namely, \[Mv= \lambda v\] In this case, we call \(\lambda \) an \(\textbf{eigenvalue}\) of \(M\) and \( v\) an \(\textbf{eigenvector}\) of \(M\) associated with eigenvalue \(\lambda\). By convention, we write \(\lambda_i(M)\) for the \(i^{th}\) largest eigenvalue of \(M\).

(lemma 1) Let one eigenvalue of \(n \times n\) matrix \(A\) be zero, say \(\lambda_n(A)=0\). Then \[ \prod_{i=1}^{n-1} \lambda_i(A)|det(B \quad v_n)|^2 = det(B^*AB)\] for any \(n \times n-1 \) matrix \(B\).

So the main result of the paper is:
(lemma 2) \[|v_{i,j}|^2 \prod_{k=1, k \neq i}^n ( \lambda_i(A) - \lambda_k(A)) = \prod_{k=1}^{n-1}(\lambda_i(A) - \lambda_k(M_j))\]
Notice that if \( Av_n = \lambda_n v_n \) then
  • \((A- \lambda_n I) v_n = 0 \)
  • \( (A - \lambda_n I) v_i = (\lambda_i - \lambda_n) v_i \)
So we conclude that the eigenvactors \(v_i\) of \(A\) with eigenvalue \(\lambda_i\) are eigenvectors of \(A - \lambda_n(A) I\) with eigenvalue \(\lambda_i-\lambda_n\)
So we consider the matrix \(A- \lambda_n(A) I\), so if we think of \(A\) in the lemma as \(A- \lambda_n(A) I\), the equation in the lemma with \(j=n\) would be written as: \[|v_{n,1}|^2 (-1)^{n-1}\prod_{k=1}^{n-1} \lambda_k(A)= \prod_{k=1}^{n-1} \lambda_k(M_1)(-1)^{n-1}\] We divide both side of equation by \((-1)^{n-1}\) to get: \[|v_{n,1}|^2 \prod_{k=1}^{n-1} \lambda_k(A)= \prod_{k=1}^{n-1} \lambda_k(M_1)\] Here, the right hand side is the deteminant of \(M_1\). We now apply lemma 1 by using \(B = \binom{0}{I_{n-1}}\) to get : \[ \prod_{i=1}^{n-1} \lambda_i(A)|det(B \quad v_n)|^2 = det(B^*AB)\] For example, using the matrix \(A\) example in the first lemma, we have: \[ B^*AB= \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 7 & 1+i & 2+2i \\ 1-i & 1 & 2 \\ 2-2i & 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2\\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \] So \(det(B^*AB)=det(M_1) \) and we notice that \(det(B \quad v_n) = v_{n,1}\) by doing cofactor expansion along the last column. So this proves the equality of equation above.

We used the fact that the determinant of a matrix is the product of its eigenvalues to expand the last equality above.
Now, we look at the \(jj\) entry of the right side of the above equality: \[ (\prod_{k=1; k \neq i}^n ( \lambda_i(A) - \lambda_k(A))v_iv_i^*)_{jj} = \prod_{k=1; k \neq i}^n ( \lambda_i(A) - \lambda_k(A)) |v_{ij}|^2 \] And this is the left hand side of the equation that we wanted to prove. And that concludes the proof.
(corollary 3) If an entry \(j\) of eigenvector \(v_i\) of a hemitian matrix \(A\) is \(0\), \(v_{i,j} = 0\), then one of the eigenvalues of the minor \(M_j\) must match \(\lambda_i(A)\).
We use the equation in lemma 2, we have: \[|v_{i,j}|^2 \prod_{k=1, k \neq i}^n ( \lambda_i(A) - \lambda_k(A)) = \prod_{k=1}^{n-1}(\lambda_i(A) - \lambda_k(M_j))\] But if \(v_{i,j} =0 \) well then the righthand side \(\prod_{k=1}^{n-1}(\lambda_i(A) - \lambda_k(M_j)) \) is \(0\) and so for some \( k \in [n-1]\), \(\lambda_i(A) - \lambda_k(M_j)=0\) and so \( \lambda_i(A) = \lambda_k(M_j)\)
