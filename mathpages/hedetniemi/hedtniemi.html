Introduction

Hedetniemi conjecture in his PhD thesis that the chromatic number of the categorical product of two graph is the minimum of the chromatic number of the two.
Given two graphs \(G=(A,B),H=(X,Y)\), their categorical product(also called tensor product), denoted by \(G \times H\) is defined as: \[ V(G \times H)=A \times X \] \[ E(G \times H)= \{((z,x),(c,v)| (z,c) \in B, (x,v) \in Y \} \]
Example, \(K_3\times P_3\), where \(K_3\) is the complete graph on \(3\) vertices \(\{D,E,F\}\) and \(P_3\) is the path graph on three vertices \(\{A,B,C\}\):
Let \(H\), and \(G\) be graphs. The \(\textbf{exponential graph}\) of \(G\) with base \(H\) is denoted \(H^G\), and is it is defined as: \[ \begin{gather*} V(H^G):=V(H)^{V(G)}\\ E(H^G):=\{(u,v)| \forall (x,y) \in E(G), (u(x), v(y)) \in E(H)\} \end{gather*} \]
If \(H = K_c\), then we can think of the vertices of \(K_c^G \) as colorings of vertices in \(G\) with \(c\) colors where two colorings in \(V(K_c^G)\) are adjacent if they color adjacent vertices in \(G\) with different colors. We denote these types of exponential graphs as \(\mathcal{E}_c(G)\).

Given graphs \(G,H\), the \(\textbf{strong product}\) \(G \boxtimes H\) is defined as follow: \[\begin{gather*} V(G \boxtimes H)=V(G) \times V(H)\\ E(G \boxtimes H) = \{ ((u,v),(x,y)) |((u,v),(x,y)) \in E(G \times H) \text{ or } ((u,v),(x,y)) \in E(G \square H) \} \end{gather*} \]

The \(\textbf{girth}\) of a graph is the length of its smallest cycle.
For example, the girth of \(P_3 \boxtimes K_3\) is \(3\) as it contains a triangle say\(CE-AE-BE\).
Given a graph \(G\), its \(\textbf{radius}\), denoted by \(r(G)\), is the smallest distance among all largest distances between pairwise vertices in the graph.
The \(\textbf{fractional chromatic number}\) of a graph \(G\) is the infimum over all \(m \in \mathbb{N}\) of \[\chi_f(G) := \inf_m \frac{G \boxtimes K_m}{m}\]
Let \(G\) be a graph, \(n \in \mathbb{N}\) a constant and \(k \in [n]\), we let \(\bar{k} \in V(\mathcal{E}_n(G)) \) be the contant mapping that maps all vertices to the color \(k\). A \(\textbf{suited colouring}\) of \((K_n)^G\) is a proper \(n\) coloring \(\phi: (K_n)^G \to K_n \) such that \(\phi(\bar{k})=k \).

(Observation 1) Given a graph \(G\) and a proper suited coloring \(f\) of \(V(\mathcal{E}_n(G))\) for some \(n \in \mathbb{N}\), Then \(f(\phi) \in Im(\phi) \).
Consider a color \(b \notin Im(\phi) \), then \( (\bar{b}, \phi) \in E(\mathcal{E}_n(G))\). And so \( \phi\) could not have been given the color \(b\) in a proper suited coloring of \(V(\mathcal{E}_n(G))\).
(Shitov). Given a suited colouring \(\phi: (K_n)^G \to [n]\). We know that \(\phi(\alpha) = b \in Im(\alpha)\). So some vertex of \(G\) is colored \(b\) by \(\alpha\). If any \(b\) colored map \(\beta \in (K_n)^G\), maps a vertex in \(N[v] \subset V(G)\) to color \(b\), then we say \(b\) is \(v\)-\(\textbf{robust}\).

Now, we analyse why \(M := \{\mu_{q+1},...,\mu_c\} \) form a clique(complete graph).
If we have \((w,i),(u,j)\) where \(dist(u,v) \geq 3\) and \(dist(w,v) \geq 3\) then they receives different colors under two differrent maps \(\mu_{\alpha}, \mu_{\beta}\) as one of them reveives color \(\alpha\) and the other receives color \(\beta\).
If we have \((w,i),(u,j)\) where \(dist(u,v) = 2\) and \(dist(w,v) \geq 3\) then they receives different colors under two differrent maps \(\mu_{\alpha}, \mu_{\beta}\) as one receives colors in \(\{1,...,q\}\) and the other receives color in \(\{q+1,...,c\}\).
There are no edges between \( (u,i),(w,j)\) if \(dist(u,v)=2\) and \(dist(w,v)=2\) because the path from \(u\) to \(v\), the path from \(w\) to \(v\) and edge \((u,v)\) form a cycle of length less than \(6\) which contradict the girth requirment of \(G\).
If we have \((w,i),(u,j)\) where \(dist(u,v) = 2\) and \(dist(w,v) = 1\) then they receives different colors under two differrent maps \(\mu_{\alpha}, \mu_{\beta}\) as one receives colors in \(\{1,...,q\}\) and the other receives color in \(\{q+1,...,2q\}\).
There are no edges between \( (u,i),(w,j)\) if \(dist(u,v)=1\) and \(dist(w,v)=1\) as they could form a cycle of length \(3\) in \(G\) violating the girth requirment.
If we have \((w,i),(v,j)\) where \(dist(w,v) = 1\) then they receives different colors under two differrent maps \(\mu_{\alpha}, \mu_{\beta}\) as one receives colors in \(\{1,...,q\}\) and the other receives color in \(\{q+1,...,2q\}\).
Hence vertices in \(M\) form a clique.
Now, as \(c = \lceil 3.1 q \rceil \), \(|M| \geq \lceil 3.1q \rceil -q >2q \). So as all vertices in \(M\) receives different colors, there must be \(\tau \in \{q+1,...,c\}\) such that \(\Lambda(\mu_{\tau}) \notin \{1,...,2q\}\). So since the only possible colors in \(Im(\mu_{\tau})\) that could be \(\tau\) is in vertices \((u,i)\) where \(dist(u,v) \geq 3\), we have \(\tau\) is not \(v\)-robust. Consider the ratio of number of no \(v\)-robust colors over \(c-2q-1\): \[ \frac{\sqrt[n]{n^3(3.1q)^{n-1}}}{c-2q-1} = \frac{\sqrt[n]{n^3(3.1q)^{n-1}}}{1.1q} = \frac{n^3(3.1q)^{n-1}}{1.1^n q^n} \to 0 \text{ as } q \to \infty\] So for large enough \(q\), there must be a color\(\sigma \notin \{1,...,2q,\tau\}\) that is \(v\)-robust. Consider a map \(\nu : G \boxtimes K_q \to \{1,...,c\}\): \[\begin{cases} \nu(g,i) = \tau , \text{ if } g \in N(v) \\ \nu(g,i) = \sigma , \text{ if } dist(v,g) \geq 2 \end{cases} \] \(\nu\) is adjacent to \(\mu_{\tau}\) but has the same color as \(\mu_{\tau}\). So that is a contradiction, so \(\chi(\mathcal{E}_c(G \boxtimes K_q)) > c \).

The counterexample

Consider graph \(G\) such that girth of \(G\) is at least \(6\) and \(\chi_f(G) > 3.1\). Existance of such graph \(G\) is garanteed by classical result. Then we let \(c = \lceil 3.1 q \rceil\) and take \(q\) large enough so that \[\chi( \mathcal{E}_c(G \boxtimes K_q)) >c \text{(by claim 3) and } \chi((G \boxtimes K_q)) \geq q \cdot \chi_f(G) >c \] However, \[ \chi(\mathcal{E}_c(G \boxtimes K_q) \times (G \boxtimes K_q))=c \] The last claim is because \(\chi(G \times \mathcal{E}_c(G))=c \) and this is true because of the coloring: \[ \phi: V(G \times \mathcal{E}_c(G)) \to [c] \text{ by } \phi((u,f)) \to f(u) \]
